History and Culture of the Peoples

An essential journey to understand the roots and strength of the peoples who form our Brazil.

Before 1500 — Original Peoples and Ancestral Knowledge

The territory we know today as Brazil has been inhabited for millennia, with archaeological evidence dating back more than 60,000 years. The Original Peoples were not homogeneous groups, but complex civilizations with vast linguistic and cultural diversity (such as the Tupi, Jê, Aruaque, and Caraíba groups, among others). They developed sophisticated systems of social organization, a deep knowledge of nature, and advanced environmental management techniques, such as the creation of Indian Black Earth (*Terra Preta de Índio*). Their ancestral knowledge and spiritual traditions constitute an intangible heritage that persists and resists to this day, challenging the narrative of a "discovery" of empty lands.

16th Century — Resistance and Encounters

The 16th Century marks the beginning of Portuguese colonization and a period of profound transformations and violence. The arrival of forced Africans, via the slave trade, established Brazil as the largest slave territory in the West, receiving about 40% of the 12.5 million trafficked Africans. This period was characterized by violent encounters and immediate resistance. Indigenous Peoples faced genocide, enslavement, and the loss of their territories, while Africans resisted from the moment they boarded the slave ships. The struggle for the preservation of cultures and identities became a continuous act of insubordination and survival.

Quilombos — Spaces of Freedom and Culture

Quilombos represent the most organized and emblematic form of Black resistance in Brazil. They were communities of resistance formed by runaway enslaved people, but also by Indigenous people and poor whites, who settled in hard-to-access locations. More than mere refuges, they became spaces of freedom and culture, where African traditions and autonomous social organization were preserved and recreated. The Quilombo dos Palmares, led by Zumbi, is the ultimate symbol of this struggle, representing the quest for a free and just society.

19th and 20th Centuries — Movements for Recognition and Rights

The 19th Century was marked by the struggle for the abolition of slavery, culminating in the *Lei Áurea* (Golden Law) of 1888, which, however, did not guarantee the social and economic integration of the freed people. The 20th Century saw the emergence and consolidation of more structured movements for recognition and rights. Indigenous and Quilombola communities intensified the struggle for territorial demarcation and the recognition of their constitutional rights, especially after the 1988 Federal Constitution, which recognized the social organization, customs, languages, beliefs, and traditions of Indigenous peoples, and guaranteed the definitive ownership of their lands to the Quilombo remnants.

Today — Current Voices and Continuous Resistance

The struggles for the preservation of culture, territory, and rights remain strong. Current Voices of Indigenous and Quilombola leaderships gain national and international prominence, denouncing violence, deforestation, and the delay in land demarcation. The continuous resistance of these communities is fundamental for the maintenance of biodiversity and for the construction of a fairer and plural Brazilian society, keeping their traditions alive and reaffirming their historical and cultural importance.

Leaderships and Activists

Meet the voices fighting for the rights of the original peoples

Sônia Guajajara

Guajajara/Tentehar People. APIB coordinator, fighting for the demarcation of Indigenous lands and the rights of original peoples.

Davi Kopenawa

Yanomami People. Shaman and activist who fights for the protection of the Amazon Rainforest and the rights of the Yanomami people.

Txai Suruí

Paiter Suruí People. Young activist fighting for climate justice and Indigenous rights at COP30.

Célia Xakriabá

Xakriabá People. Teacher and activist who fights for Indigenous education and land demarcation.

Raoni Metuktire

Kayapó People. Chief who has been fighting for the protection of the Amazon Rainforest for decades.

Daniel Munduruku

Munduruku People. Author, educator, and activist who promotes Indigenous culture through education.

Alessandra Korap Munduruku

Munduruku People. Internationally awarded activist for the defense of the environment.

Ailton Krenak

Krenak People. Thinker and activist who reflects on the relationship between Indigenous peoples and nature.

Kumi Naidoo

Global activist who works with Indigenous peoples in the fight for sustainability.
